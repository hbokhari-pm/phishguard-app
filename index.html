What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cybercrime where attackers impersonate legitimate organizations to steal sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, credit card details, or other personal data. These attacks typically occur through email, text messages, phone calls, or fake websites.

Cybercriminals use psychological manipulation techniques to create a sense of urgency, fear, or excitement to trick victims into taking immediate action without thinking critically about the request.

Common Phishing Techniques

Email Phishing Red Flags
  • Urgent or threatening language ("Your account will be closed!")
  • Generic greetings ("Dear Customer" instead of your name)
  • Suspicious sender addresses that don't match the claimed organization
  • Poor grammar, spelling errors, or awkward phrasing
  • Requests for sensitive information via email
  • Unexpected attachments or links
  • Mismatched or suspicious URLs when hovering over links

Types of Phishing Attacks

Spear Phishing: Targeted attacks aimed at specific individuals or organizations, often using personal information to appear more credible.

Whaling: High-value targets like executives or celebrities, often involving business email compromise (BEC) attacks.

Smishing: Phishing via SMS/text messages, often involving fake delivery notifications or account alerts.

Vishing: Voice phishing through phone calls, where attackers impersonate customer service or technical support.

Pharming: Redirecting users from legitimate websites to fake ones through DNS manipulation.

Protection Strategies

  • Always verify the sender's identity through independent means before acting on requests
  • Hover over links to preview URLs before clicking, and type addresses manually when possible
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on all important accounts
  • Keep software, browsers, and security systems updated
  • Be skeptical of urgent requests, especially those involving money or sensitive information
  • Use official websites and apps instead of clicking links in emails or messages
  • Report suspicious emails to your IT department or email provider
  • Regularly monitor your accounts for unauthorized activity
  • Educate yourself and others about current phishing trends and techniques

What to Do If You've Been Phished

If you suspect you've fallen victim to a phishing attack:

  • Change passwords immediately for any compromised accounts
  • Contact your bank or credit card companies if financial information was involved
  • Report the incident to relevant authorities and your organization's IT department
  • Monitor your accounts and credit reports for suspicious activity
  • Run antivirus scans if you downloaded any malicious files
  • Document the incident for future reference
